Many team fights are lost after the team has already created a good opening. One player shoots the vulnerable target, another launches that target to safety, and the third chases someone else behind a wall.

The cure is not a longer combo. It is making three players solve the same problem for a few seconds.

## Choose targets by opportunity

The best target is not always the character at the top of a tier list. Prioritize the opponent who is:

- Separated from their team.
- Exposed without nearby cover.
- Missing a major movement option.
- Already pressured by a teammate.
- Attempting a revive, heal, or long animation.
- Occupying the position your team needs.

A dangerous backline character may deserve attention, but crossing through two healthy enemies to reach them can turn correct target priority into bad positioning.

## Use one ping to create a local advantage

Ping the target or their location before committing. Then watch whether a teammate responds.

If two players attack the same isolated opponent, the team creates a temporary two-versus-one even while the full fight remains three-versus-three. That short numerical advantage is often enough to force a knockdown, guard break, escape, or rescue attempt.

Do not spam several different enemies. One useful signal is easier to follow than a map full of alarm bells.

## Understand what a knockdown changes

Launch and knockdown effects can interrupt pressure and may give the opponent a protected recovery window. Exact behavior varies by move and update, but the strategic tradeoff is consistent:

- A knockdown can stop an enemy attack.
- It can peel an enemy away from a teammate.
- It can create time to heal, reload, or reposition.
- It can also interrupt allied damage and move the target toward safety.

Before using a launching move, decide whether you want **damage now** or **space now**.

If teammates are landing safe pressure, avoid displacing the target without a reason. If your team needs breathing room, the displacement may be the most valuable result.

## Use launchers as punctuation

A launching attack is strongest when it ends a sentence:

- It confirms a down.
- It removes a diver from your teammate.
- It throws the target into the storm or a worse position.
- It creates the reset your team needs.
- It interrupts a dangerous ability.

It is weaker when used automatically in the middle of allied pressure.

Some battle styles are built around repeated displacement, so this is not a universal ban. The question is whether the launch improves the team state.

## Build a simple team-fight rhythm

Use this sequence:

1. **Spot:** identify the exposed opponent.
2. **Signal:** ping once and move into supporting range.
3. **Pressure:** use reliable attacks before expensive commitments.
4. **Convert:** spend crowd control or burst when the target loses an exit.
5. **Recheck:** locate the other enemies before chasing.
6. **Reset:** heal, reload, and reposition after the advantage.

The recheck prevents a common throw: all three teammates following one low-health enemy while the other two opponents attack from behind.

## Do not confuse damage with progress

Damage creates value only when it changes the fight. Useful outcomes include:

- Forcing an enemy out of position.
- Consuming their healing or movement.
- Protecting a teammate.
- Securing a down or KO.
- Taking high ground or safe cover.

If both teams trade damage and fully reset, the fight may have accomplished nothing except attracting another squad.

After each pressure sequence, ask: **What did we gain?**

## Set a chase limit

Decide how far the team can chase before the pursuit begins. Useful limits include:

- One building.
- One mobility cycle.
- Until the target reaches their team.
- Until the storm route becomes unsafe.
- Until a new enemy appears.

When the limit is reached, return to the team. A low-health opponent who escapes has still lost time and healing. Chasing for another minute may give their teammates the isolated target they wanted: you.

## Practice target handoffs

In your next matches, focus on this one skill:

1. Attack the target a teammate is already pressuring.
2. Stop when that target becomes protected or unreachable.
3. Turn the camera and identify the next exposed enemy.
4. Ping the handoff.
5. Rejoin the team before beginning another chase.

Good team fighting is not permanent focus on one opponent. It is coordinated attention that moves faster than the enemy can recover.

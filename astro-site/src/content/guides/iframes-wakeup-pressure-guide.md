Knockdowns create a small decision game. The recovering player wants to use temporary protection to escape or counterattack. The attacker wants to cover the first vulnerable action without wasting every resource into invincibility.

Community players often call deliberate pressure across that recovery window **frame pressure** or **frame pressing**. The term is useful, but the exact timing and protection rules are patch-sensitive. Verify them in Training after major updates.

## The core idea

After some knockdowns, the defender receives a protected recovery window. During that sequence:

- Immediate attacks may fail to damage the recovering player.
- Movement or an ability can make the defender's next path predictable.
- Acting too early may expose the defender before they reach safety.
- The attacker can lose resources by firing blindly into protection.

The expert skill is not memorizing one timing. It is recognizing which player benefits from waiting.

## Defender: do not autopilot the escape

The obvious response is often to press a mobility skill immediately. Experienced opponents expect that.

Before acting, identify:

- Which enemy is watching your recovery.
- Whether their punish is already active.
- The nearest cover that can be reached without a skill.
- Whether a neutral recovery changes the attacker's timing.
- Whether your teammate can interrupt the pressure.

Sometimes the safest option is to delay, reposition during the protected movement, and act only after the attack passes. Sometimes immediate escape is correct. The mistake is choosing before reading the screen.

## Attacker: pressure the decision, not the invincibility

Do not empty your kit into a protected target. Use an option that:

- Covers the likely escape direction.
- Remains active long enough to test an early action.
- Can be canceled or abandoned safely.
- Does not leave you exposed to the defender's teammates.
- Preserves a second response if the defender waits.

The goal is to make the defender reveal a choice. If they spend movement, punish the exit. If they remain patient, keep position rather than forcing a bad attack.

## Build a two-option wake-up plan

Before the opponent stands, choose:

**Option A: cover the early action.** Aim at the most likely dash, jump, teleport, or attack route.

**Option B: punish patience.** Hold a safer angle, reload, or reposition so the defender cannot simply wait and reset for free.

If your plan has only one timing, a patient opponent can defeat it by doing nothing.

## Read character intent

Battle styles advertise what they want after recovery:

- A mobile character often wants elevation or distance.
- A close-range brawler may challenge immediately if armor or interruption is available.
- A defensive character may place a wall, shield, trap, or area denial tool.
- A support may move toward a teammate rather than directly away from you.

Do not reduce this to a rigid matchup chart. Watch what the player did after the previous knockdown. Habits are more reliable than stereotypes.

## Know when to abandon wake-up pressure

Leave the setup when:

- Another enemy gains an angle on you.
- Your own team needs help.
- The target recovers beside strong cover.
- Your punish requires every escape resource.
- The storm or a third party changes the fight.
- Missing the attack would leave you surrounded.

Wake-up pressure is a small advantage, not permission to ignore the battle royale.

## Defensive mix-ups

Rotate among several responses:

- Neutral recovery into cover.
- Delayed movement after the attacker commits.
- Immediate escape when the route is genuinely open.
- A defensive or interrupting action when the opponent overextends.
- Movement toward teammates instead of away from danger.

Do not use every option equally. Use the response that attacks the opponent's current expectation.

## Offensive restraint

An expert attacker should be comfortable gaining no immediate damage. If standing over the target forces them to spend a valuable escape, your team can punish the next position instead.

Preserve:

- One movement option.
- One reliable interrupt or tracking tool.
- Awareness of the other two enemies.

The opponent's wake-up is not the entire fight.

## Training drill

Use Training or a willing practice partner:

1. Record which actions preserve or end recovery protection in the current build.
2. Test immediate action, delayed action, and neutral recovery.
3. Practice aiming at the exit instead of the downed body.
4. Test one persistent pressure option and one patient response.
5. Repeat near a wall, on a roof, and in open ground.

Write down behavior, not damage numbers. Timing and decision rules remain useful longer than a combo total.

The player who acts first does not automatically win the wake-up. The player who understands why they are acting usually does.

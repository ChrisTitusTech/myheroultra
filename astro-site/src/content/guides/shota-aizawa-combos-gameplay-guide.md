This guide covers **Shota Aizawa's original Technical battle style**, not the Flow Runner alternate style.

Aizawa is a control character disguised as a combo character. His best plays do not begin with forcing the longest possible string. They begin with catching an exposed opponent, disabling the right Quirk at the right time, and leaving before the rest of the enemy team can punish him.

His execution is demanding, but beginners only need three dependable actions:

1. Pull an exposed enemy with grounded Alpha.
2. Use Beta to move, finish a short confirm, or disengage.
3. Use Erasure briefly to interrupt an important action, then move or attack.

## Quick beginner game plan

Play from one movement away from cover, preferably with some elevation nearby.

- **Before a fight:** keep Alpha ammunition available and identify a wall, roof, or ledge you can grapple.
- **When teams meet:** look across the fight rather than diving into its center. Pull enemies who are healing, reviving, climbing, aiming, or attacking your teammate.
- **After a successful pull:** use one short follow-up and reassess. Do not spend every skill on one target while two enemies aim at you.
- **When pressured:** grapple away with airborne Alpha or use Beta to change direction. Gamma can contest close pressure and swat projectiles, but it is not permission to stand still in crossfire.
- **When your team commits:** Erase the opponents who can interrupt your teammate's attack, revive, or escape.

The beginner objective is not maximum damage. It is to create one unfair moment for your team, survive, and do it again.

## What each skill is for

### Alpha — Binding Cloth: Capture

Grounded Alpha restrains an opponent and pulls them toward Aizawa. Pressing Alpha again completes the pull immediately. This is your main opening tool.

Airborne Alpha behaves differently: it sends Aizawa toward the impact point. Use it on terrain as a grapple or connect it with an enemy for a flying kick. The same ammunition therefore powers both offense and survival.

Practical rules:

- Aim at players during committed animations instead of trying to win every neutral exchange with raw reactions.
- Pull from an angle where the target cannot immediately place their whole team behind them.
- Do not empty Alpha for damage. Keep a charge when you may need to reach cover or elevation.
- Walls, roofs, trees, signs, and other solid surfaces are movement resources.

Alpha gains size at levels 4 and 9. Those breakpoints make catches more forgiving.

### Beta — Powerhouse Kick Barrage

Beta dashes in the aim direction while delivering repeated kicks and a finishing hit. It is a follow-up, a chase tool, and a movement reset—not merely a damage button.

Beta gains a second charge at level 4 and reloads faster at level 9. The second charge is the important early breakpoint because it lets you spend one charge without surrendering every escape option.

Recent Season 17 footage shows Beta producing unusually high and sometimes inconsistent multi-hit results. Treat the current damage ceiling as patch-sensitive. Build your decisions around the dash, charge count, and reliable finisher rather than assuming every target will take the same total damage.

### Gamma — Binding Cloth: Serpent

Gamma swings the cloth continuously while held. It deals repeated hits and can swat away incoming projectiles.

Use Gamma for:

- Punishing an opponent who has entered your close space.
- Extending a confirmed pull.
- Protecting a narrow approach from ordinary projectiles.
- Creating hit stun long enough to release early into a follow-up.

Holding the entire Gamma is not always correct. The full animation leaves you committed, can grant the target time to recover, and gives other enemies a stationary target. Experienced Aizawa players often release before the final hit when they want to continue pressure.

Gamma's important upgrades are levels 4 and 9, which improve its area and reload speed.

### Special Action — Erasure

Erasure seals the Quirk Skills of opponents visible on your screen and can interrupt active Quirk Skills. It affects what Aizawa can see, so camera placement and line of sight matter.

Use it as an interrupt, not a staring contest:

1. Put the important enemy or enemies clearly on screen.
2. Activate Erasure as they commit to a valuable Quirk Skill.
3. Hold only as long as the interruption or team opening requires.
4. Cancel it and immediately reposition, pull, or let your teammate convert.

Strong Erasure moments include:

- Stopping a movement skill while your team focuses its user.
- Interrupting a large area attack before it controls the fight.
- Denying Quirk pressure around a revive or recovery attempt.
- Turning off an escape while a teammate closes distance.
- Protecting a low teammate from a pursuer's Quirk Skills.

Erasure does not make Aizawa invulnerable. Enemies can still move, use normal melee, break line of sight, or let another teammate attack from outside your view.

## Recommended level order

For a beginner-friendly all-purpose route:

1. **Alpha to level 4** for the first size breakpoint.
2. **Beta to level 4** for the second charge.
3. **Gamma to level 4** for improved area and reload.
4. **Alpha to level 9** for the strongest, most forgiving catch tool.
5. **Gamma to level 9** for better control uptime.
6. **Beta to level 9** last.

This route values consistency and keeps the full kit usable.

An aggressive current-patch alternative is Alpha 4, Beta 4, then max Beta earlier. That route leans on Beta's present multi-hit output, which is more balance-sensitive and less reliable across target movement, terrain, and connection quality.

## Combo notation

- **Ground Alpha** means the restraint and pull version.
- **Air Alpha** means the grapple/flying-kick version.
- **Melee 1** means only the first normal melee hit.
- **Short Gamma** means release before the final hit instead of holding the entire skill.

These are practical routes, not universal guarantees. Guard state, knockdown protection, target size, elevation, walls, latency, teammate hits, and prior stun all change what is true.

## Beginner combos

### 1. Safe team confirm

**Ground Alpha pull → teammate attack**

This is Aizawa's most important “combo.” Pull the target into your team's line and avoid adding a hit that knocks them away from a stronger teammate attack.

Use it when your teammate is already looking at the target. Ping first when possible.

### 2. Basic personal confirm

**Ground Alpha pull → Beta**

Aim Beta through the target rather than sharply across them. This is easy to understand and gives you movement at the end of the sequence.

Use it when you need a quick punish. Avoid it when Beta is your only available exit.

### 3. Extra-hit confirm

**Ground Alpha pull → Melee 1 → Beta**

Delay just enough for the pulled opponent to arrive, tap the first melee hit, then Beta. Do not mash the whole melee string.

The melee makes this less universally true than the direct Alpha-to-Beta route. Some characters, spacing, or connection conditions allow an escape. Practice the timing before treating it as automatic.

### 4. Anti-pressure punish

**Short Gamma → Beta**

Start Gamma when a close enemy commits into your space. Release after you have confirmed repeated hit stun, then aim Beta along their movement.

This is safer than always holding Gamma to its final hit, but the release timing must be learned in Training.

## Intermediate and advanced routes

### 5. Gamma release into Alpha

**Gamma → release before the final hit → Ground Alpha**

The timing is the point. Release too early and the enemy can escape; hold too long and the last Gamma hit may end the window. A useful visual cue is to stop on the second-to-last part of the Gamma sequence, then bind as the opponent remains suspended in hit stun.

This route is sensitive to elevation and online delay. If it is inconsistent in a match, end with Beta or disengage instead of repeatedly gambling the punish.

### 6. Pull into controlled Gamma

**Ground Alpha pull → Melee 1 → Short Gamma → optional Beta**

This is a high-commitment single-target route. The Beta extension is conditional: use it only if the target has not entered knockdown protection and the enemy team cannot punish your stationary Gamma.

Stopping after Short Gamma is often the better ranked decision.

### 7. Air Alpha conversion

**Jump → Air Alpha through the target/impact point → aerial or grounded follow-up**

Air Alpha can produce multiple contact moments depending on the angle and where Aizawa lands. It is useful against grounded opponents and as a surprise approach from elevation.

Do not build your beginner game plan around exact air-bounce strings. Slopes, ledges, target position, and camera angle can change the landing. First learn to use Air Alpha as accurate movement, then add follow-ups.

### 8. Guard-break conversion

**Confirmed string that breaks Guard Points → re-check target state → continue**

Some longer Aizawa routes become possible when a hit breaks the target's guard and changes the normal knockdown flow. Do not pre-buffer the entire sequence. Watch for the guard-break cue, then decide whether Alpha, Gamma, or Beta still has a clean line.

This is an advanced reaction, not a fixed memorized string.

## Timing and input discipline

### Do not mash after Alpha

The target travels toward you. Inputting the follow-up too early can send a melee or Beta past the pull point. Watch the cloth retract and time the next attack for the opponent's arrival.

### Release Gamma deliberately

There are three useful Gamma lengths:

- **Tap/short hold:** quick close-range interruption.
- **Controlled hold:** enough hits to confirm a follow-up.
- **Full hold:** maximum area duration when the control itself matters more than extending.

Use the shortest version that achieves the current goal.

### Aim Beta before pressing it

Beta follows the aim direction. Small camera errors become large misses once the dash starts. Center the route through the target, and consider where the animation will leave you.

### Preserve an exit

Before committing, count:

- Remaining Alpha charges.
- Remaining Beta charges.
- Nearby grapple surfaces.
- Enemies who are not currently disabled or occupied.

If all four answers are bad, the combo is not available even if the first hit lands.

## Movement practice

Aizawa's movement becomes reliable when you stop treating every grapple as “go directly to that point.”

### Grapple high, then redirect

Aim airborne Alpha above your desired landing point. The extra height gives you time to turn the camera, choose a roof, or spend Beta laterally.

### Use slopes for low, fast travel

Air Alpha behaves differently around angled terrain and can carry Aizawa smoothly without the same landing shape as a flat wall. Test common ramps and roofs in Training so you know which surfaces preserve momentum.

### Alternate resources

Do not spend all Alpha charges consecutively by habit. A common escape rhythm is:

**Air Alpha → adjust camera/fall → Beta → Air Alpha**

The pause and Beta movement give Alpha time to reload while making your path harder to track.

### Move across sightlines

Grappling straight away from a ranged enemy keeps you in their reticle. Move diagonally across cover, change elevation, and break line of sight before healing.

## Team-fight priorities

Aizawa should usually attack the fight's structure rather than its nearest body.

Prioritize:

1. An enemy using or preparing a revive.
2. A ranged carry standing still to fire.
3. A mobile enemy who has just spent an escape.
4. An opponent diving your vulnerable teammate.
5. A target your teammate can immediately punish after a pull.

Avoid dragging a durable brawler directly onto a low-health teammate. A successful pull can still be a bad decision.

When your team has a large area attack or strong burst, your highest-value contribution may be holding Erasure for one second and letting them do the damage.

## Matchup considerations

### Against projectile-heavy teams

Use cover first and Gamma second. Gamma can swat projectiles in front of you, but multiple firing angles or area attacks can overwhelm a stationary defense. Pull shooters during their firing animation and leave after the confirm.

### Against armored or close-range pressure

Do not meet every approach with raw melee. Create spacing, use Gamma to contest predictable entry, and save Erasure for the Quirk Skill that lets the opponent force contact or escape.

### Against highly mobile characters

Let them spend movement before attempting Ground Alpha. Erasure can remove their next Quirk-based escape, but only while they remain visible. Coordinate the focus instead of chasing alone across open terrain.

### Against ground-control characters

Elevation is defense. Use Air Alpha and rooftops to avoid fighting entirely on their preferred surface. Descend when you have a confirmed target, not because the fight happens to be below you.

## Common beginner mistakes

- Holding Erasure until the gauge is exhausted with no teammate ready to punish.
- Pulling an enemy while standing in full view of the enemy team.
- Spending every Alpha charge on offense and discovering there is no exit.
- Holding Gamma to completion every time.
- Using both Beta charges for damage before checking who is aiming back.
- Practicing maximum-damage strings before learning a reliable pull, short confirm, and disengage.
- Chasing a low target so far that Aizawa loses the sightlines that make Erasure valuable.

## Ten-minute Training routine

1. **Two minutes:** grapple between walls, roofs, and slopes without touching the ground unnecessarily.
2. **Two minutes:** Ground Alpha pull into direct Beta from several ranges.
3. **Two minutes:** Ground Alpha pull into Melee 1 into Beta without mashing.
4. **Two minutes:** hold Gamma for different lengths and practice releasing into Alpha.
5. **Two minutes:** begin each combo with one escape charge reserved, then leave the area immediately.

Once those actions are repeatable, practice against moving bots and add Erasure before the opening. A combo that works only against a stationary Training target is not ready for ranked play.

## Final checklist

Before each engagement, ask:

- Who can my team punish if I pull them?
- Which enemy Quirk is worth interrupting?
- Where is my next grapple point?
- Am I spending my last movement charge?
- Can the other two enemies see me during this combo?

If you can answer those questions while fighting, you are playing Aizawa's real game: control the opponent's options, create a short advantage, and disappear before they can answer.

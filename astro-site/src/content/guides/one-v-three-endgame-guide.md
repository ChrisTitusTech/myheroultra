A true one-versus-three is supposed to be unfair. You do not improve the odds by attacking all three players more confidently. You improve them by making sure the enemy team cannot use all three players at the same time.

The clutch plan is: steal time, split paths, punish one mistake, and reset before the other two can trade.

## Change the win condition

Your first objective is not a KO. It is creating a playable sequence.

Look for one of these states:

- One enemy climbs while two remain below.
- A fast enemy chases beyond support range.
- One opponent stops to heal or revive.
- Terrain blocks two players while one keeps vision.
- Another team or the storm forces the squad to divide attention.

Only then does the fight briefly become a one-versus-one.

## Refuse the center of the circle

The middle gives three enemies room to surround you. Prefer the edge of playable space, where terrain or the storm removes approach angles.

Good positions offer:

- A wall or drop on one side.
- More than one exit.
- Vertical separation.
- A short path to new cover.
- Enough vision to see who is chasing first.

Do not trap yourself in a dead end. A position is defensible only if you can leave it after the enemy commits.

## Split pursuit paths

Move through terrain that forces different characters to arrive at different times:

- Roofs with multiple levels.
- Buildings with separate doors and windows.
- Drops that can be reversed with mobility.
- Corners that break tracking.
- Narrow passages that punish stacking.

The purpose is not to run forever. It is to create a gap between the first pursuer and the rest of the team.

When that gap appears, turn briefly and deal safe pressure. Leave before support arrives.

## Identify the impatient player

Most teams have one player who believes the numbers advantage makes every chase safe. Watch for the opponent who:

- Spends all movement to reach you.
- Takes the shortest route without cover.
- Attacks before teammates have line of sight.
- Repeats the same recovery or escape.
- Continues chasing after the team stops.

That player is your opening. Punish the overextension, not necessarily the most dangerous character.

## Reset after every successful exchange

One good hit does not authorize a full commitment. After winning an exchange:

1. Break line of sight.
2. Reload or recover charges.
3. Check the storm.
4. Locate all remaining enemies.
5. Heal if the route is genuinely hidden.

A clutch is usually several small wins separated by resets. Staying for “just one more hit” is how a temporary advantage becomes a trade you cannot afford.

## Use the enemy's revive as pressure

If you secure a down, the other players must choose between:

- Protecting the downed teammate.
- Chasing you.
- Attempting a revive.
- Repositioning for the storm.

Do not automatically stand over the body. Control the routes around it and punish whichever responsibility the enemy neglects.

If guarding the down exposes you to both living players, leave. The down has already bought time and split attention.

## Spend resources in survival order

Prioritize:

1. An escape or line-of-sight break.
2. Enough healing for one real reset.
3. A reliable punish for the first isolated pursuer.
4. Optional damage or utility beyond that.

Do not consume the final escape to land speculative damage. In a one-versus-three, survival is the resource that lets every other resource reload.

## Let the storm negotiate

The final circle can reduce the enemy's ability to spread out, but it can also remove your exits.

Track:

- Which side closes first.
- Which pieces of cover remain playable.
- Whether the enemy must cross open space.
- Whether you can rotate before the team establishes a firing line.

Move early when the next safe position is obvious. Move late only when enemy attention or terrain will hide the transition.

## When another team is alive

You are not obligated to finish anyone else's fight. Let stronger teams damage one another, then enter for a short conversion.

Maintain enough distance that:

- Neither team can turn on you instantly.
- You can see knockdowns or major movement being spent.
- Your escape does not cross through the winning team.

The moment both teams notice you, leave. Being the third party is useful; becoming the shared target is not.

## A clutch decision loop

Repeat:

1. Where are all three enemies?
2. Which route makes them arrive separately?
3. What resource will I need to leave?
4. Is one opponent punishable now?
5. Where is my next reset?

If you cannot answer the first question, gather information instead of attacking.

You will not win every one-versus-three. The expert goal is to stop donating a simultaneous fight and force the enemy team to beat you one decision at a time.

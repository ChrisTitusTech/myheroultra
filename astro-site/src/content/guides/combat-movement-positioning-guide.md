Good movement is not maximum speed. It is reaching useful space while keeping enough resources to survive the next decision.

Popular improvement and movement guides repeatedly return to the same lesson: players become much harder to eliminate when they stop crossing open ground without a route, stop spending every mobility charge at once, and keep the camera pointed toward the danger they expect.

This guide is character-agnostic. Exact charge counts, reload times, and movement interactions can change, so test your battle style in Training.

## Plan three points, not one destination

Before moving, identify:

1. **Departure cover:** the wall, roof edge, doorway, or terrain that currently protects you.
2. **Transition cover:** the object that can interrupt enemy vision halfway through the move.
3. **Arrival cover:** the position from which you can fight, heal, or continue rotating.

If the route has no transition cover, ask whether you can cross from a higher angle, wait for enemy attention to move, or spend one mobility tool to shorten the exposed section.

Running directly at the final destination makes your path easy to read. Moving through two pieces of cover gives you somewhere to change direction.

## Treat mobility as an economy

Divide movement tools into three jobs:

- **Entry:** gaining an angle or joining a favorable fight.
- **Correction:** dodging a punish or changing elevation.
- **Exit:** breaking vision and leaving the engagement.

Using every charge for entry means borrowing from the other two jobs. Unless the attack will immediately decide the fight, preserve one reliable exit.

This matters even on highly mobile characters. Fast players are often eliminated because they travel farther from their team before discovering they have no charge left for the return trip.

## Move the camera before the character

Camera control is part of movement. Before leaving cover:

- Check the high ground above your route.
- Sweep the side from which a third party could arrive.
- Locate both teammates.
- Identify the nearest doorway, roof lip, or corner that can break tracking.

While retreating, alternate between looking at the route and checking the pursuer. You need enough information to know whether the enemy is still chasing, cutting you off, or abandoning the pursuit.

Do not stare backward for the entire escape. Running into a wall is a remarkably efficient way to help the enemy.

## Use height to reduce the number of threats

High ground is valuable because it changes who can attack you at the same time. A roof edge or raised platform can:

- Hide most of your body while you gather information.
- Force close-range enemies to spend movement reaching you.
- Create a drop route that does not require an ability.
- Let you pressure one side without exposing yourself to the entire team.

Height is not automatically safe. Leave when several enemies gain your level, when the storm removes your drop route, or when staying makes your position predictable.

## Break vision before healing

Distance alone does not make a reset safe. Many attacks can follow a straight retreat.

A better escape sequence is:

1. Cross behind solid cover.
2. Change elevation or direction.
3. Check whether the enemy follows.
4. Move again if your location remains obvious.
5. Heal only after the pursuer must guess.

The goal is to remove information. A player who knows exactly which corner you used can pre-aim the heal interruption.

## Spend natural movement first

Use ordinary options when the route allows them:

- Drop from a roof instead of spending a downward movement skill.
- Climb behind cover rather than scaling the visible face of a building.
- Use doors and interior turns to break tracking.
- Slide or sprint during low-risk travel.
- Save the character-specific escape for the dangerous section.

Natural movement is renewable. Quirk charges are not.

## Make your retreat difficult to copy

A pursuer wants you to move in a straight, predictable line. Make the chase expensive:

- Turn after breaking line of sight.
- Change elevation at corners.
- Pass near teammates who can punish the chase.
- Move through terrain that favors your effective range.
- Stop chasing your own escape route if the enemy has already cut it off.

Do not zigzag randomly in the open. Direction changes are strongest when cover hides the moment you choose them.

## A ten-minute movement drill

Enter Training with one battle style and repeat:

1. Cross a small area while keeping one mobility charge unused.
2. Reach a roof using the least expensive route.
3. Drop, break vision twice, and choose a different exit.
4. Turn the camera during movement without colliding with terrain.
5. Practice returning to a teammate marker after an imaginary attack.

Then play three matches with one goal: finish every engagement with an exit tool available.

You do not need to become faster. You need to become harder to predict.
